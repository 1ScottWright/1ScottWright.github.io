Alogorithmic Art

August 4, 2018

The local instructor of our DSI course, Caroline Schmitt, once again provided the inspiration the bit of work that led to this blog post. She used her expertise in natural language processing to come up with a way to demonstrate Markov chains. In particular, her lecture demonstrated how to come up with an "Austen-y" text generator by using the "Sense and Sensibility" corpus found in the nltk.corpus library.

In essence, we created a dictionary that has keys equal to each unique word in the corpus and the values for each of those keys equal to a list of the words that immediate follow the key. Once we created this dictionary, it was then easy to generate text of a fixed length by starting with one word, looking up the list of words that follow the word from the dictionary, and then randomly selecting one of those entries.

I decided to extend this concept to look a 2-grams (ie, two words that are found together in the text). I then created a dictionary with the 2-grams that follow each of the occuring two-grams. My question, though, was how frequently does the text self-replicate? I decided to first pick a starting point and generate trials with a length of 4, 6, 8, 10, etc. words. Each trial was 10,000 sets of random text

I found that the text self-replicated (ie, generated the same sequence of words as Austen) 100% of the time for my starting point and phrases of 6, 8, and 10 words. After that, the drop off in success rate was steep, 2.1% for 12 words, 0.5% for 14, and 0.2% for 16 words.

Then it was clear that I wasn't going to get any rest that evening until I also played around with random starting points. I decided to repeat a simlar process 200 times, each time with a randomly selected starting point and a text lenght of 12 words. The result was a mean success rate of 38.9% and a standard deviation of 18.5%
